Why your smoke alarms need testing (and how often)

Coastline Electrical·30 April 2026·4 min read

A smoke alarm costs less than a tank of fuel but can save your family's lives, as long as it works when it matters. The trouble is that alarms quietly fail, batteries die, and units pass their use-by date without anyone noticing. Here's what you need to know.

Test every month

Press the test button on each alarm once a month. It takes seconds and confirms the alarm and its battery are working. If an alarm doesn't sound, it needs a new battery or replacement. Set a recurring reminder on your phone, the first of the month is easy to remember.

Replace alarms every 10 years

Smoke alarms don't last forever. The sensor degrades over time, and most alarms have a use-by date printed on them, usually around 10 years from manufacture. An expired alarm may not detect smoke reliably even if the test button still beeps. If yours are undated or old, replace them.

Photoelectric is the recommended type

Photoelectric smoke alarms detect smouldering, smoky fires faster than older ionisation alarms, and have fewer nuisance alarms from cooking. They're the recommended standard, and what we install as standard.

Interconnection matters

If you have more than one alarm, interconnected alarms all sound together, so a fire at one end of the house wakes you at the other. In a larger or two-storey home this can make a real difference to the time you have to get out.

Rentals have stricter rules

If you're a landlord or managing agent in NSW, there are specific smoke alarm requirements you must meet, including ensuring alarms are working at the start of each tenancy. We handle rental smoke alarm compliance and provide the documentation you need.

Get it sorted

If you're not sure whether your alarms are compliant, the right type, or simply too old, book a smoke alarm check. We'll assess the locations, install photoelectric interconnected alarms where needed, and for rentals provide the compliance paperwork. It's a small job that genuinely matters.
